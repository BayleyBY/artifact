Where this leads

The six cards on this wall span a single decade — 2003 to 2013. In that time, transistor counts grew by 56×, memory by 16×, and raw compute by roughly 90×. Each generation arrived with new promises, new failures, and new rivalries.

But the arc doesn't stop here. The card that closes this collection — the GTX 760 — was the generation where engineers started asking a different question. Not just "how fast can it render?" but "what else can it compute?"

The GPU that learned to draw shadows in 2003 became, a decade later, the chip that would teach machines to see. The same parallel architecture — thousands of tiny cores doing the same operation at once — turns out to be exactly what you need to train a neural network.

Every modern AI system — image generators, language models, the tools recommending what you watch and translating what you say — runs on the descendants of cards like these. The GTX 760's Kepler architecture is a direct ancestor of the chips NVIDIA builds for AI datacenters today.

The phone in your hand carries forward the same lineage. The GPU that fits in your pocket, runs on a battery, and renders your camera's portrait mode in real time — it is, in a direct technical sense, the child of what's mounted on this wall.

These cards didn't just power games. They powered the future.
